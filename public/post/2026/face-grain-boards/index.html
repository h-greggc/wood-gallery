Wood Grain in Cutting Boards

H. Gregg Claycamp |   11 March 2026
 |  2 minutes

Grain Faces: three sides of a board

Face grain

Cutting and charcuterie boards are usually designated as one of three grain appearances: face, edge, and end. Face grain is the widest side of the board, Face grains offer natural wood growth-ring patterns that can either high contrasting curved arches (a.k.a. “cathedrals”), elipses, straight lines, curly or even “tiger” striped figures.1 In wood working, a single, wide board showing face grain is usually the prettiest presentation of the wood. Some projects benefit both artistically and structurally by having a very straight face grain, while other projects favor art of a flowing, curvy face grain over durability.

Edge grain

Edge grain is the narrower side of a flat board. Edge grain cutting boards are the most popular cutting board for home use because they are very stable, solid and they can be artistic arrangements of different species of hardwoods within one board. Edge-grain building involves many glue joints (face-to-face) between edge grain strips. Each glue joint increases the strength of the board. Edge-grain boards can be sized from small cutting board to full-sized kitchen counter tops.

End grain

End grain cutting boards are often the high-end of the cutting board options. Most end grain boards are built by cutttin, say, 2-inch cross-grain strips from endge-grain boards and then standing the strips on end before glue-up. That means that there is a huge amount of board milling and glue-up to build the edge grain board.

What causes warpage in wood?

Inadequate control of moisture content is the primary cause of warpage in wooden objects.

Face grain cutting boards carry the same risks for warpage as does decking and, e.g., face grain table tops in indoor or outdoor furniture.

however, it carries the greatest risk of warpage over time. For example, decks typically use face-grained surfaces. It’s clear that many of the boards will show “cupping” curvatures after exposure to the elements. The culprit in causing warpage is changing relative humidity!

Even if a wide, face grain cutting board can be made from a single wide board, I open apply one or more strategic cuts even in order to relieve the stress in the wood grain while also retaining the natural figure.
