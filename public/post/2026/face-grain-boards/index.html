Wood Grain in Cutting Boards

H. Gregg Claycamp |   11 March 2026

Grain Faces: three sides of a board

Face grain

Cutting and charcuterie boards are usually designated as one of three grain appearances: face, edge, and end. Face grain is the widest side of the board, Face grains offer natural wood growth-ring patterns that can either high contrasting curved arches (a.k.a. “cathedrals”), elipses, straight lines, curly or even “tiger” striped figures.1 In wood working, a single, wide board showing face grain is usually the prettiest presentation of the wood. Some projects benefit artisically and structurally by having very straight face grain, while other projects favor art over durability for random patterns of face grain.

Edge grain

Edge grain is the narrower side of a flat board. Edge grain cutting boards are the most popular cutting board for home use because they are very stable, reliable and they can be artistically assembled from different species of harrd woods within the sam board. sizeable “butcher block” styles of boards. Edge-grain building involves many glue joints (face-to-face) of edge grain strips. Each glue joint dramatically increases the strength of the board. Thus, edge-grain boards can be built to remain flat from small sizes to full kitchen counter tops.

What causes warpage in wood?

Inadequate control of moisture content is the primary cause of warpage in wooden objects.
